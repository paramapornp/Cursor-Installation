1. Walter Writes: The AI detector that tells you what to fix, not just that something's wrong
Walter Writes is the tool I'd put first in a detection workflow, and the reason isn't just the scores. It's that detection and humanization are in the same platform.

Every other detector on this list tells you whether content is flagged. Walter Writes tells you that and then lets you run the content through the humanizer without switching tools. For professional content work, that matters. The workflow is: draft, detect, humanize any sections that flag, detect again. Not: draft, open one tool, copy to another tool, go back, copy again.

On detection accuracy: Walter Writes uses the same underlying signal analysis that the leading commercial detectors use, measured against sentence rhythm, perplexity variation, and burstiness. In my testing, it's consistent across long-form B2B content, which is the content type that causes the most problems in professional contexts.

The platform also includes the humanizer that produces scores of 0% on GPTZero, 79% probability human-generated on Proofademic, and 0% on QuillBot's detector. Those are the numbers from my own testing on real client content. The detection and humanization are calibrated to work together.

Free trial: 300 words per run, no card required. Paid plans start at $8/month annual billing. For teams that need to integrate detection into a pipeline rather than use the web interface, the API is available from $49/month.

2. GPTZero: The most widely used detector, but not the strictest
GPTZero is the most commonly referenced AI detector in professional and academic contexts. It's fast, the interface is clean, and the results are readable. It's also not the strictest tool in the market.

My experience running content through GPTZero alongside Proofademic and Originality AI is that GPTZero passes content that the other two flag. The difference isn't small. Content that GPTZero scores at 15% AI can score flagged on Proofademic. That gap matters if your client or publication is using something stricter.

What GPTZero is good for: a fast first pass to identify sections that need attention. What it isn't: a sufficient signal on its own if the content is going somewhere with a real detection policy. If you're using GPTZero as your only detector, you're working with incomplete information.

GPTZero has a free tier for basic scanning and a paid plan for higher volume. It's worth having in your toolkit as one of several detectors, not as the single checkpoint before delivery.

3. Proofademic: Built for accuracy on professional and academic content
Proofademic is the detector I'd reach for second after Walter Writes. It's built specifically for academic and professional content, and it operates at the sentence level rather than just scoring the full document. That granularity is useful for understanding where in a piece the detection signals are concentrated.

The sentence-level analysis is what makes it practical for humanization workflows. Instead of knowing "this article scored 65% AI," you know which sections are driving that score. That's actionable information. You can humanize those sections specifically instead of running the whole piece through again.

Proofademic is also stricter than GPTZero on professional-register content, which makes it the more relevant test for B2B content going to clients with detection policies. If your content clears Proofademic, it's in good shape against most commercial detection tools.

For educators, the detection interface also makes it usable in an academic context where you need to document detection results clearly. That's a different use case from content production, but the tool handles both.

4. Originality AI: The detector B2B content teams should know about
Originality AI is positioned primarily at content marketing teams and agencies rather than academic use. It's one of the more accurate detectors for the kind of long-form professional content that gets flagged in B2B contexts, and it combines AI detection with plagiarism checking in a single interface.

The interface is designed for team use: you can scan pages, manage a content library, and track detection results across a project. For an agency managing content for multiple clients, that organizational layer is useful.

On accuracy: my experience with Originality AI is that it catches content that GPTZero passes. It's not identical to Proofademic in where it draws the line, so running both gives you better coverage of the detection landscape your content will actually face.

Originality AI is a paid tool, priced by credits. There's no meaningful free tier for production use. Worth evaluating if you're running content at volume and need a detector that's calibrated for professional-register writing.

5. QuillBot AI Detector: Useful as a quick first pass
QuillBot has added detection to its platform alongside the paraphrasing tools. The detector is functional for a fast pass on content that you want a rough read on. It's not the tool I'd rely on as the final checkpoint before delivery, but it's convenient if you're already in the QuillBot workflow.

The detection accuracy on professional long-form is lower than Proofademic or Originality AI. It's better suited for identifying obvious AI patterns in short content than for doing a thorough pass on a 1,500-word B2B article.

Use it as a first-pass indicator. Don't use it as your only checkpoint.

6. aichecker.tech: A functional free option for basic testing
For basic detection testing without a paid subscription, aichecker.tech is worth knowing. It handles short to medium-length content and gives you a readable score. It's not what I'd use for final delivery review, but for testing a humanization pass or checking a quick piece before it goes anywhere, it's a useful free option.

The limitation is the same as most free detection tools: it's not calibrated for professional-register content the way Proofademic or Originality AI is. On short consumer-facing copy, it performs reasonably. On long-form B2B writing with professional vocabulary, the accuracy is less reliable.

What a detection score actually means
A number people get stuck on: what percentage AI detection is actually a problem?

There's no universal answer, because different detectors use different scales and different clients have different thresholds. A 40% score on GPTZero and a 40% score on Proofademic don't mean the same thing. The numbers aren't comparable across tools.

What matters is whether the content clears the specific detector your client or platform is running at the threshold they've set. That's the test. Running one detector and getting a low score doesn't tell you whether you'd clear a different detector at a stricter threshold.

The practical workflow I use: run Walter Writes detection first to identify sections that need attention, humanize those sections, run Proofademic as a second check on anything going to a client with explicit detection requirements. If both pass, deliver. If Proofademic flags something specific, go back to that section.

The practical question
The question behind all of this is: what does your content need to clear, and what's the most reliable way to know it will?

For most professional content work, the answer is: Walter Writes for detection and humanization together, Proofademic as a second pass for anything with strict client requirements, and Originality AI if you're running a content operation where you need team-level tracking and audit records.
